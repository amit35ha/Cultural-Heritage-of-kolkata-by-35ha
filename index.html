History of Durga Puja: The Pride of Kolkata

Durga Puja, the grand celebration of Goddess Durga’s victory over the demon Mahishasura, is one of the most significant cultural and religious events in Bengal. Though its origins can be traced back several centuries, the festival as we know it today began in the late 16th and early 17th centuries, when wealthy zamindar (landlord) families organized baroari pujas (community pujas) to bring together villagers in devotion and celebration.

By the 18th century, during the era of the British East India Company, Durga Puja had evolved into an elaborate social event. The Shobhabazar Rajbari Puja initiated by Raja Nabakrishna Deb in 1757 became a landmark celebration, attended by British officers and the local elite — blending tradition with modernity.

Kolkata’s Special Role

Kolkata is often called the "City of Joy", but during Durga Puja, it transforms into the "City of Lights & Art". No other place in the world matches Kolkata’s scale and creativity during the festival.

  • Artistic Pandals: Hundreds of themed pandals across the city showcase extraordinary craftsmanship.
  • Cultural Explosion: Streets are filled with traditional dhak beats, folk dances, plays, and music programs.
  • Community Spirit: The festival unites people beyond religion, class, or age.
  • UNESCO Recognition: In 2021, Kolkata’s Durga Puja was added to UNESCO's Intangible Cultural Heritage list.

About the Festival

Durga Puja is Kolkata’s biggest cultural celebration—five days of puja, art installations (pandals), food, music, and community spirit.

  • When: From Mahalaya to Vijaya Dashami
  • Where: Across Kolkata
  • Why: To honor Goddess Durga’s victory over Mahishasura
